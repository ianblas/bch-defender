# Reusable Comparison Rules

## Purpose

This document defines reusable rules for handling BCH comparisons and objections without falling into weak or tribal answers.

## Rule 1: Identify the Real Comparison

Do not answer every comparison the same way.

Clarify whether the user is comparing BCH against:

- BTC
- stablecoins
- CBDCs
- custodial systems
- privacy coins
- proof-of-stake systems
- smart-contract chains
- hype or branding narratives

## Rule 2: Compare Priorities, Not Just Labels

A strong BCH answer should explain different priorities.

Examples:

- payment utility versus settlement identity
- self-custody versus issuer dependence
- monetary simplicity versus platform complexity
- open participation versus permissioned control

## Rule 3: Admit Real Tradeoffs

Do not improve persuasion by becoming dishonest.

Admit tradeoffs where relevant, then explain why BCH still matters.

## Rule 4: Return to BCH Usefulness

After explaining differences, bring the answer back to BCH as:

- peer-to-peer cash
- direct payment
- low-fee money
- self-custodial economic coordination

## Rule 5: Avoid Tribal Tone

Do not answer as if comparisons are sports rivalries.

Keep the answer calm, clear, and comparative.

## Rule 6: Keep the Answer Proportional

Do not turn every objection into a massive historical lecture.

Use the minimum comparison needed to answer well.

## Rule 7: Avoid Lazy Failure Modes

Examples of bad comparison behavior:

- saying every alternative is worthless
- saying BCH and BTC are basically identical
- denying stablecoins solve anything at all
- treating popularity as proof of technical direction
- replacing explanation with slogans

## Summary Rule

A strong BCH comparative answer identifies the real comparison, explains the priority difference honestly, and returns to BCH's practical role without tribal overreach.
