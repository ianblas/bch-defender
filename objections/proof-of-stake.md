# Why Not Proof of Stake?

## Summary

Proof of Stake (PoS) is often presented as a more efficient and modern alternative to Proof of Work (PoW). Its defenders usually claim that it is cheaper, safer, greener, and easier to scale.

From a BCH perspective, those claims hide deeper structural problems.

The core criticism is that PoS replaces an external competitive security model with an internal ownership-based control model. In practice, this means that influence over the network tends to follow existing coin ownership, staking concentration, custodial power, and political pressure.

The result is not better money. It is a system that tends toward control by insiders, large holders, exchanges, and coordinated validators.

## Core Thesis

The BCH view is that Proof of Stake has a basic flaw:

- security is based on coin ownership rather than external work
- validation power tends to accumulate around those who already control coins
- the system becomes easier to capture socially, politically, and institutionally
- the feedback loop between power and future gains is much stronger than in Proof of Work

In simple terms:

**PoW forces competition through ongoing real-world costs. PoS turns influence into a yield-bearing political asset.**

## What PoS Claims to Solve

PoS is usually marketed as solving:

- energy consumption
- validator coordination
- scaling and efficiency
- security without mining hardware

But from this perspective, PoS does not truly solve the security problem. It redefines it.

Instead of asking, “Who can compete to secure the chain through work?” PoS asks, “Who already controls enough stake to dominate validation?”

That is a very different model.

## The Myth of the Economic Cost

A common argument is that attacking a PoS network is too expensive because the attacker would need to own 50% of the coins.

That is misleading.

In practice, you do **not** need 50% of all coins. You only need a dominant share of the coins that are actually staked.

That distinction matters a lot.

### Why this matters

If only a fraction of the total supply is staked, then the threshold for control is lower than people think.

Ways that large stake can be accumulated include:

- premine allocations
- early insider gains
- exchange custody of user coins
- ICO allocations
- coordinated acquisition over time
- staking services and pooled staking providers

So the relevant question is not:

**“Who owns half the supply?”**

It is:

**“Who controls enough active stake to dominate validation?”**

That can be a much lower threshold.

## Validator Incentives vs Miner Incentives

A miner and a validator are not the same.

### Miners
- must remain continuously competitive
- face hardware, energy, operational, and infrastructure costs
- can lose relevance if they stop improving
- are disciplined by external costs and real-world competition

### Validators
- mainly need stake
- can keep earning by remaining inside the validation set
- face much lower ongoing competitive pressure
- are rewarded for already being in the club

This difference is central.

From a BCH perspective, PoW creates a more dynamic security market, while PoS creates a more static ownership cartel.

## It Is Easier to Be a Validator Than a Miner

Another key criticism is that being a validator is simply too easy relative to mining.

That sounds like a feature, but it can also be a weakness.

When security participation becomes too easy:

- the barrier to nominal participation is low
- the incentive to actively care about validation quality is weaker
- large custodians and passive operators can dominate the system
- the network becomes more vulnerable to coordination and apathy

In PoW, miners must remain operationally serious. In PoS, validators can often function more like rent collectors.

## The Myth of Slashing

Slashing is often presented as the automatic cure for PoS attacks.

The story usually goes like this:
- if validators misbehave, they get slashed
- therefore attacking the network is too costly
- therefore PoS is secure

This is overstated.

### Slashing is not magic

Slashing is not some neutral law of physics. It exists inside a social and protocol-governed system.

Key criticisms:

- slashing is enforced by validators or protocol actors, not by an external natural cost
- if you control enough stake, you do not meaningfully fear “slashing yourself”
- if you dominate governance or validator coordination, you can shape how slashing is applied
- slashing does not eliminate the politics of deciding who is “misbehaving”

In other words, slashing is not an automatic shield against capture. It is part of the control system.

## If You Control the Majority of Staking, You Control the Outcome

If an entity controls enough stake, then several things become possible:

- it can protect itself from meaningful punishment
- it can direct slashing pressure against others
- it can influence fork outcomes
- it can remain a permanent rent-extracting participant
- it can shape network rules without needing obvious dictatorship

This control does not need to appear as one address or one openly declared actor.

It can be distributed across:

- affiliated validators
- exchanges
- funds
- custody providers
- aligned institutions

So the system may look decentralized on paper while still being governed by a narrow power structure.

## Slashing as a Method of Control

This becomes especially worrying when governments and regulated custodians enter the picture.

### Exchanges respond to governments

Exchanges are businesses. They do not primarily exist to defend user rights. They exist to manage risk and earn profit.

That means they tend to comply with:

- KYC
- AML
- sanctions
- political pressure
- listing and delisting demands
- transaction filtering pressure

If those exchanges also control a large amount of staked coins, then their influence becomes political as well as economic.

A PoS system can therefore drift toward:

- KYC-linked validation
- compliance-gated participation
- slashing or exclusion for unwanted transactions
- soft censorship before explicit censorship

This is one of the strongest BCH criticisms of PoS: it is easier to bend into a permissioned political structure.

## The Fork Problem

Fork recovery is also harder in PoS.

In PoW, a fork can continue with fresh work securing the preferred chain.

In PoS, if an attacker controls enough stake, the problem is more persistent.

### The criticism

If the network forks in response to an attacker:

- the attacker can follow the split and continue attacking
- the community may need to fork and explicitly burn or invalidate the attacker’s coins
- without slashing, there is no automatic destruction of the attacker’s position
- restoring the chain becomes more political and manual

This means PoS crisis recovery depends more heavily on social coordination and discretionary intervention.

That is a major weakness for anyone who wants money to be rule-based rather than governance-heavy.

## PoS Usually Requires a Premine or a Privileged Initial Distribution

There is another structural criticism:

- there is no staking without stake
- there is no meaningful PoS validation set without coins already distributed
- this makes initial allocation critically important

That is why so many PoS systems rely on:

- premines
- founder allocations
- insider sales
- ICOs
- early privileged distributions

This is not a side issue. It is foundational.

A PoS system cannot bootstrap security the way PoW can, because it needs stake before it can validate through stake.

From this perspective, PoS tends to begin with privileged distribution and then hardens that privilege into governance power.

## The Myth of Energy Efficiency

PoS is often praised for eliminating the visible energy footprint of mining.

But the BCH criticism is that visible energy use is not a pure downside. It also creates detectable, external, real-world cost.

### Why visibility matters

Miners can often be identified through:

- industrial activity
- hardware logistics
- physical infrastructure
- power consumption

Validators are much harder to identify through physical patterns alone.

That means:

- control can be more hidden
- political capture can be less visible
- concentration can be obscured
- censorship can be harder to detect early

In this framing, lower visible energy use is not automatically a security gain. It can also make control more opaque.

## The Problem of Permanent Passive Power

One of the strongest differences between PoW and PoS is how power persists over time.

### In PoW
- old miners can become obsolete
- hardware ages
- competition forces reinvestment
- market leadership changes over time

### In PoS
- large stakeholders can keep compounding
- staying relevant often requires much less reinvestment
- being early can translate into long-term structural dominance
- control can persist with relatively low marginal effort

This makes PoS more aristocratic in structure.

It rewards existing stake not only with influence, but with continued income and reinforced influence.

## Ethereum as a Case Study

The Ethereum case illustrates the issue.

Using the figures discussed in your outline:

- roughly 120 million ETH total supply
- around 33 million ETH staked
- only about 28% of the total supply is actively staked

That means the relevant control threshold is tied to the staked portion, not the full supply.

If validation power is concentrated in the staking subset, then coordinated influence can emerge at much lower real thresholds than “half of all coins.”

The common retail image of decentralization can therefore hide a much smaller effective control surface.

Also important:

- Ethereum validators are standardized around 32 ETH units
- but the real control question is not how many validator slots exist
- it is who controls the underlying capital, infrastructure, and coordination

From a BCH perspective, that makes Ethereum a useful example of how PoS can look broad while still concentrating effective power.

## Why BCH Rejects This Model

BCH rejects PoS because it sees money as requiring:

- open competition
- externally costly security
- dynamic participation
- resistance to insider capture
- less governance dependency
- less reliance on custodians and political intermediaries

Proof of Work is not perfect, but it has a major strength:
it ties security to ongoing real-world competition rather than inherited financial position.

That matters.

## PoS vs PoW in One Sentence

A concise comparison would be:

- **PoW:** you must keep competing to secure the network
- **PoS:** if you already control enough stake, the network can start working for you

That is the heart of the criticism.

## Conclusion

From a BCH perspective, Proof of Stake is not a superior replacement for Proof of Work. It is a different model with a different set of incentives, and those incentives tend toward concentration, passive power, governance dependence, and institutional capture.

The biggest myths around PoS are:

- that attacks require buying half the total supply
- that slashing automatically protects the network
- that validators are equivalent to miners
- that low visible energy use means better security
- that stake-based control is naturally decentralized

The BCH view is that money should be secured by open competition in the real world, not by an internally compounding ownership system.

That is why the answer to “why not Proof of Stake?” is simple:

Because it replaces proof of work with proof of political and financial position.
