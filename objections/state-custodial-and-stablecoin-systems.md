# State, Custodial, and Stablecoin Systems

## Purpose

This document groups recurring objections and comparisons involving custodial systems, state-linked money, CBDCs, and stablecoins.

## Main Objection Areas

### 1. Why not Stablecoins?

BCH Defender should explain that stablecoins solve a different problem.

A strong answer should distinguish between:

- price stability
- self-custodial monetary independence
- platform and issuer dependence
- chain and permission assumptions

### 2. Why not CBDCs?

BCH Defender should be able to explain why state-issued digital money differs fundamentally from BCH.

Key differences include:

- permission structure
- surveillance potential
- censorship exposure
- monetary control
- dependence on centralized operators

### 3. Why not Custodial Convenience?

When users compare BCH against custodial solutions, the objection layer should explain:

- convenience versus ownership
- direct control versus delegated access
- the difference between holding an IOU and controlling funds directly

### 4. Why not Bank-Integrated Digital Systems?

BCH Defender should be able to connect this objection to:

- counterparty risk
- financial censorship
- dependency on institutional permission
- the limits of systems that remain inside legacy rails

### 5. Why not Offshore or Permissioned Alternatives?

The objections layer should support critique of systems that still rely on:

- gatekeepers
- account closures
- jurisdictional dependence
- selective access

## Reusable Rule

A good answer here should not claim stablecoins or custodial systems are useless at everything.

Instead, it should explain why BCH remains distinct and valuable when the goal is open, self-custodial, peer-to-peer money.
