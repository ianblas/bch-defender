# Why SegWit Was the Wrong Direction for Bitcoin

## Summary

From a BCH perspective, SegWit was not a real solution to Bitcoin’s scaling problem. It was a workaround that preserved BTC’s small-block policy while introducing extra complexity, breaking a simpler model of on-chain scaling, and helping redirect Bitcoin away from peer-to-peer electronic cash.

The core criticism is not merely that SegWit was technically imperfect. It is that SegWit became part of a broader political and architectural choice:

- keep the base layer constrained
- avoid direct blocksize scaling
- move users toward second-layer systems
- redefine Bitcoin away from simple on-chain payments

This is why BCH rejects the idea that SegWit was the right answer.

## Core Idea

The BCH view is that Bitcoin’s main problem was straightforward: if more people wanted to use the network, the network needed more transaction capacity.

SegWit did not solve that problem directly.

Instead, it tried to gain limited extra room while preserving the anti-scaling philosophy of BTC. In that sense, SegWit is criticized not just as a technical design, but as a strategic detour away from the original peer-to-peer cash model.

## What SegWit Was Supposed to Do

SegWit, or Segregated Witness, is commonly described as a change that separated signature data from the traditional transaction structure and changed how block capacity was counted.

Supporters presented it as useful for several reasons:

- reducing certain forms of transaction malleability
- allowing more effective block capacity under the new accounting system
- making later second-layer systems easier to build

From a BCH perspective, the problem is that even if those goals had some narrow merit, they did not justify the broader cost of refusing direct on-chain scaling.

## The BCH Critique of SegWit

## 1. It avoided the real scaling solution

The most important criticism is that SegWit was adopted instead of simply raising transaction capacity directly through larger blocks.

From a BCH perspective, the honest solution was:

- allow more transactions on-chain
- keep Bitcoin useful as money
- preserve simple direct payments

SegWit did not do that. It preserved the small-block mindset while offering a more complicated workaround.

## 2. It made Bitcoin more complex

A recurring BCH criticism is that SegWit added architectural complexity where a simpler capacity increase would have been cleaner.

Complexity matters because it:

- makes the system harder to reason about
- makes software and infrastructure more awkward
- increases the gap between ordinary users and protocol specialists
- adds friction instead of removing it

From this point of view, SegWit is not a sign of elegant engineering. It is a sign that BTC chose an indirect fix to avoid admitting the obvious need for on-chain scaling.

## 3. It changed Bitcoin to defend a political choice

From a BCH perspective, SegWit cannot be understood only technically. It must also be understood politically.

BTC had a choice:

- scale the base layer more directly
- or preserve the small-block model and redesign Bitcoin around it

SegWit is criticized as part of the second path. It helped defend the narrative that Bitcoin should not scale on-chain for mass payments, and that users should instead adapt to a more constrained system.

## 4. It supported the move toward second-layer dependence

Another central BCH criticism is that SegWit helped prepare BTC for a second-layer future, especially Lightning-style narratives.

That means SegWit is often seen as one step in a chain of decisions that pushed Bitcoin toward:

- off-chain payment dependence
- routing complexity
- custody and semi-custody
- fragmented user experience
- a weaker connection between base-layer usage and miner incentives

From a BCH perspective, this is one of the deepest problems with SegWit: it was not just a code change. It was part of a different philosophy of Bitcoin.

## 5. It did not preserve Bitcoin as simple peer-to-peer cash

Bitcoin originally aimed to function as peer-to-peer electronic cash.

The BCH position is that ordinary users should be able to:

- receive coins directly
- send coins directly
- use the chain itself for normal payments
- avoid unnecessary architectural layers

SegWit did not move Bitcoin in that direction. It moved BTC toward a more abstract and layered design where the base chain became less suited for broad direct payments.

## Transaction Malleability and the BCH View

SegWit supporters often emphasize malleability fixes as a major benefit.

From a BCH perspective, the answer is not necessarily that malleability was unimportant. The answer is that the chosen remedy was tied to a broader anti-scaling agenda.

The BCH critique is therefore:

- fixing one technical issue does not justify weakening the monetary model
- a technical improvement is not automatically a good strategic direction
- the real question is whether the overall path preserved Bitcoin’s original usefulness

BCH’s answer is that it did not.

## Capacity Without Honest Scaling

One reason BCH criticizes SegWit so strongly is that it allowed BTC supporters to claim they had improved capacity without embracing honest on-chain scaling.

From a BCH perspective, this was misleading.

It let people say:

- blocks are effectively bigger now
- scaling is happening
- no larger-block approach is needed

while still preserving a model in which BTC remained too constrained for broad global payment use.

That is why BCH sees SegWit as a half-measure used to avoid the actual solution.

## The Wrong Incentive Direction

Bitcoin’s long-term security depends on real network activity.

From a BCH perspective, that means a healthy Bitcoin model should encourage:

- many on-chain transactions
- broad direct use
- low-fee participation by many users
- strong alignment between miner incentives and network utility

SegWit did not strengthen that model. Instead, it supported a system where the base layer stayed more limited and the future was pushed toward second layers and financial workarounds.

That weakens the direct connection between usage and security.

## BCH vs SegWit Thinking

### SegWit/BTC approach

- preserve strict base-layer limits
- modify transaction structure to squeeze more room out of the same political constraint
- prepare the ground for second-layer scaling
- accept more complexity in exchange for avoiding blocksize growth

### BCH approach

- scale on-chain directly
- keep payments simple at the base layer
- preserve low fees for ordinary users
- let higher layers exist only when they complement the base chain rather than replace it

This is why the disagreement is not merely technical. It is about what Bitcoin is supposed to be.

## Why BCH Rejected the SegWit Path

BCH rejected the SegWit path because it saw that path as incompatible with the original purpose of Bitcoin.

From the BCH perspective, SegWit represented:

- avoidance of direct scaling
- extra complexity
- support for a second-layer-centric future
- growing distance from peer-to-peer electronic cash

BCH instead chose a different direction:

- keep Bitcoin usable on-chain
- preserve simple payments
- support direct transaction capacity growth
- maintain Bitcoin as money, not just settlement infrastructure

## Short Answer

If someone asks, “What is the problem with SegWit?” a BCH-style short answer would be:

SegWit was a workaround used to preserve BTC’s small-block policy instead of solving the scaling problem directly. It added complexity, supported a move toward second-layer dependence, and helped redirect Bitcoin away from simple peer-to-peer electronic cash.

## Conclusion

From a BCH perspective, the problem with SegWit is not only technical. It is historical, political, and economic.

SegWit became part of a broader choice to keep BTC constrained and to replace direct on-chain cash usage with a more layered, more complex, and less user-friendly model.

That is why BCH sees SegWit not as the moment Bitcoin was saved, but as one of the moments Bitcoin was pushed further away from its original purpose.
