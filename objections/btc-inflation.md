# Why BTC Ends Up Needing Inflation

## Summary

From a BCH perspective, BTC's long-term design problem is not accidental. It is the result of repeatedly blocking the changes that would have preserved Bitcoin as scalable peer-to-peer electronic cash.

The argument is simple:

- BTC was intentionally constrained
- useful scaling reforms were blocked
- fees were pushed upward
- everyday usage was pushed away from the base layer
- miner incentives became weaker over the long term
- the remaining path is either real scaling or some form of inflation

This is why the claim is that **Peter Todd is right about the consequence**, even if BCH supporters reject his preferred solution.

## The Core Claim

The BCH criticism is not that inflation is good.

It is that BTC has been pushed into a corner where, if it refuses to scale, inflation starts to look like the only remaining way to sustain miner incentives over the long run.

In other words:

- if BTC does not scale
- and if fee revenue is not enough
- and if subsidy keeps falling
- then the system either weakens or tries to preserve security through inflation

That is the trap.

## Why Start by “Congratulating” Peter Todd

The sarcastic BCH framing is that Peter Todd deserves “congratulations” because he successfully helped stop Bitcoin from scaling.

The episode frames him as someone who understood the consequences of limiting BTC and followed that logic consistently. In the transcript, the hosts argue that if BTC does not scale, then inflation eventually appears as one of the only remaining paths, and they explicitly say Peter Todd sees those as the two roads: either scalability or inflation. 

From that perspective, the joke is:

- he helped keep BTC constrained
- he helped make fees worse
- he helped increase friction
- and now he points to inflation as the answer

So the BCH reading is:

**he correctly identifies the consequence of the policy path he helped defend.**

## “He Helped Stop Bitcoin”

The argument presented in the episode is that BTC was supposed to scale by allowing more transactions through larger blocks, in line with the original peer-to-peer cash vision.

The transcript describes the older mainstream Bitcoin view as straightforward: if you want more transactions, you allow larger blocks, because blocks contain transaction history and larger blocks allow more direct usage. It also describes Peter Todd as having argued in 2013 that this path should be stopped and the limit should remain. 

From the BCH perspective, the practical result was:

- the 1 MB mentality remained dominant
- transaction throughput stayed constrained
- fees became structurally higher
- useful everyday usage was discouraged
- BTC drifted away from the original cash model

That is why BCH advocates often summarize the result as:

**1 MB + RBF + rising friction = a broken incentive path**

## Blocking Useful Reform While Letting Useless Changes Through

Another core criticism is that the BTC side repeatedly rejected reforms that could have improved the network meaningfully, while permitting changes that did not solve the core scaling problem.

This framing usually includes:

### Rejected or blocked in practice
- meaningful block size growth
- scalable on-chain transaction capacity
- reforms that would preserve direct payment usability

### Mentioned as failed or politically blocked proposals
- SegWit2X
- drivechains

### Criticized as not solving the actual problem
- Taproot

The BCH argument is not necessarily that every rejected reform was perfect. It is that BTC systematically refused to solve the core issue: preserving Bitcoin as useful peer-to-peer cash at scale.

So the complaint is:

- useful reforms are blocked
- symbolic or side-path changes are tolerated
- the real economic problem remains untouched

## The Real Problem: Miner Incentives

Bitcoin needs miner incentives to work.

This point is central.

The system cannot survive forever by appealing to ideology, branding, or the hope that price will always go up. It needs a durable economic basis for security.

The BCH interpretation of Satoshi's incentive model is that Bitcoin was meant to reward:

- network interaction
- transaction volume
- real usage
- actual economic activity

Not just passive speculation.

The episode transcript explicitly emphasizes the original vision of Bitcoin as something that replaces fiat with money that works without a third party and supports fast, cheap payments. It also argues that today's BTC community moved away from that original goal. 

From a BCH perspective, that matters because a network built for real cash usage has a path toward activity-based security. A network built mainly for speculative holding does not.

## Why Price Appreciation Cannot Save BTC Forever

A common BTC assumption is that miner security can keep being protected because the price will continue rising.

The BCH objection is that this cannot be treated as a permanent solution.

### Why not?

Because over time:

- the base gets larger
- more capital is needed to move the price meaningfully
- upward volatility becomes harder to sustain
- doubling becomes less trivial
- relying on price alone becomes less realistic

The argument in your notes is that “everyone is already in BTC” in the sense that most of the obvious large capital pools already understand it, track it, or are exposed to it. Whether or not that statement is literally absolute, the strategic point is clear:

**the bigger BTC gets, the harder it becomes to rely on repeated price expansion as a substitute for real economic utility.**

So if BTC security depends on:

- declining subsidy
- insufficient fee growth
- and ever-larger price appreciation

then the model becomes fragile.

## Why the Fee Market Is Not Enough Under the BTC Approach

BTC defenders often say that fees will replace subsidy.

The BCH response is that this only works if the network is useful enough to generate a broad and durable fee base.

But BTC intentionally limits base-layer activity.

That creates a contradiction:

- the chain is too constrained for broad global payment use
- everyday activity is pushed off-chain or out of the system
- fee generation depends on a narrower class of on-chain users
- subsidy keeps falling anyway

So the question becomes:

**How does a deliberately constrained network produce enough sustained fee income to secure itself indefinitely?**

From the BCH perspective, it probably does not.

And once that becomes obvious, inflation starts appearing as the fallback.

## Inflation as the Final Escape Hatch

This is the heart of the critique.

If BTC refuses to scale, and if fee revenue is not enough, then inflation becomes politically tempting.

Why?

Because inflation can temporarily preserve miner income without solving the real underlying problem.

In that sense, inflation is not the plan of a healthy system. It is the emergency patch of a system that refused to align utility with security.

The BCH view is:

- scaling was the honest answer
- inflation is the desperate answer

## The Deeper Incentive Argument

The original Bitcoin design creates a relationship between:

- people using the network
- transactions happening on the network
- miners being rewarded for securing the network
- the network becoming stronger through actual economic relevance

BCH argues that this is the healthy loop.

BTC breaks that loop by:

- constraining throughput
- raising fees
- discouraging direct usage
- pushing activity elsewhere
- weakening the link between network use and miner incentives

Once that link is broken, speculative price growth has to carry too much of the system.

That is not sustainable forever.

## BTC's Two Remaining Choices

From this perspective, BTC eventually faces two real choices:

### 1. Scale
Allow enough real activity on-chain to create a broader economic base for miner security.

### 2. Inflate
Preserve miner incentives artificially when fee income and real usage do not fully support the system.

The BCH position is:

- scaling is the honest path
- inflation is the admission of failure

## Why BCH Sees This Differently

Bitcoin Cash takes the opposite approach.

It aims to preserve:

- low fees
- direct on-chain payments
- broad transaction activity
- merchant usability
- alignment between usage and security

That means BCH sees security not as something that should be carried mainly by number-go-up speculation, but by actual participation in the network.

From that perspective, the solution is not inflation.

The solution is to make Bitcoin useful enough that activity itself helps secure the system.

## Conclusion

The argument that BTC will end up needing inflation is a criticism of the path BTC chose after rejecting scalable peer-to-peer cash.

The BCH view is that Peter Todd correctly identifies the consequence, even if his preferred solution is wrong.

If BTC keeps:

- blocking useful scaling
- depending on high fees
- pushing away real usage
- and assuming price will always compensate

then it will eventually face a hard truth:

**a Bitcoin that refuses to scale drifts toward inflation.**

That is why BCH treats inflation not as a surprise, but as the predictable end point of BTC's anti-scaling strategy.
