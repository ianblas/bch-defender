# Smart-Contract, PoS, and Throughput Narratives

## Purpose

This document groups recurring objections and comparisons involving proof-of-stake systems, smart-contract platforms, high-throughput chains, and alternative performance narratives.

## Main Objection Areas

### 1. Why not PoS?

BCH Defender should explain that proof-of-stake changes the trust and power model.

A strong answer should distinguish between:

- security assumptions
- validation power structure
- monetary and governance implications
- different tradeoffs in coordination and control

### 2. Why not Smart-Contract Chains?

BCH Defender should explain that BCH is not trying to be every possible system at once.

A strong comparative answer should:

- clarify BCH's monetary and payment focus
- explain that programmability can exist without abandoning money-first priorities
- avoid acting as if generalized smart contracts automatically solve the cash problem

### 3. Why not High-Throughput Branding Narratives?

When users compare BCH against throughput-heavy chains, the objections layer should explain:

- headline throughput versus usable money
- operational tradeoffs
- infrastructure assumptions
- whether the system actually supports durable peer-to-peer cash use cases

### 4. Why not Meme / Hype / Branding Coins?

BCH Defender should be able to contrast:

- monetary seriousness versus hype cycles
- durable payment usefulness versus narrative pumps
- long-term infrastructure versus trend-driven attention

### 5. Why not Alternative Scaling Narratives?

The objections layer should support comparisons against projects that promise:

- faster scaling by changing the trust model
- lower friction through heavier centralization
- easier UX by relying more on platform control

## Reusable Rule

A good answer here should focus on tradeoffs and priorities.

Do not answer as if every alternative system must be worthless.

Explain instead why BCH treats peer-to-peer money as the central use case.
