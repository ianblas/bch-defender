# Why BCH Has Low Fees

## Summary

Bitcoin Cash keeps fees low because it is designed to support abundant on-chain transaction capacity instead of treating block space as an artificially scarce luxury good.

From a BCH perspective, low fees are not an accident and not merely the result of low usage. They are a design goal tied to the original peer-to-peer electronic cash model.

The BCH claim is that money works better when many people can transact cheaply, directly, and regularly. In that model, miner incentives come from broad economic activity and many transactions, not from pricing normal users out of the network.

## Core Idea

Fees stay low when the chain has enough usable capacity and when the system is designed to welcome transaction volume instead of constraining it.

The BCH approach is:

- increase or adapt transaction capacity
- preserve usability at the base layer
- allow many low-cost payments
- let miner incentives grow through broad activity rather than through extreme fee pressure

From a BCH perspective, that is healthier than a model where users compete for scarce block space and are forced into high fees or second-layer workarounds.

## Why Low Fees Matter

Low fees matter because money that is too expensive to move stops behaving like usable cash.

If small payments become uneconomic, then the network becomes worse for:

- everyday commerce
- merchant adoption
- remittances
- low-margin use cases
- community economies
- experimentation and application building

BCH treats this as a core monetary issue, not a cosmetic user-experience issue.

## BCH Was Built to Preserve Cheap On-Chain Payments

From a BCH perspective, Bitcoin should remain usable on-chain for normal payments.

That means BCH does not try to force users out of the base layer. It tries to preserve the base layer as the natural place for peer-to-peer electronic cash.

That design choice is one of the main reasons BCH can keep fees low while remaining aligned with its monetary purpose.

## Low Fees Are Not Just “Because Nobody Uses It”

A common criticism is that BCH fees are low only because usage is low.

The BCH response is that low fees are also a result of design.

BCH keeps fees low because it aims to maintain enough block space and transaction capacity for broad use. The point is not to create artificial scarcity and call that security. The point is to allow the network to absorb demand without destroying usability.

From a BCH perspective, if a system is supposed to be money, then it should be prepared for users rather than made hostile to them.

## Capacity Keeps Fees Low

The most direct reason BCH has low fees is simple:

**more usable block capacity means less pressure for users to bid against each other for inclusion.**

When block space is less artificially constrained:

- ordinary payments remain affordable
- fee spikes are less severe
- more transactions can coexist
- adoption does not immediately break the user experience

This is one of the clearest differences between BCH and BTC.

## ABLA Strengthens This Model

ABLA, the Adaptive Blocksize Limit Algorithm, is an important part of the modern BCH story.

From a BCH perspective, ABLA matters because it allows block capacity to adapt to real usage rather than remain locked behind a rigid political ceiling.

That supports a healthier fee environment because:

- the network can respond more flexibly to demand
- capacity can grow with real usage
- fee pressure does not have to explode just because adoption rises

This reinforces the BCH idea that scalability and low fees can coexist.

## Low Fees and Stable Spending

Low fees also matter when BCH is used as practical spendable money, including in workflows where users care about relatively stable purchasing behavior.

If someone wants BCH to function in real spending, then using it must remain cheap enough that the fee itself does not become the main event.

From a BCH perspective, a money system that charges too much to spend becomes worse as money, even if it remains attractive to speculators.

## Low Fees and Merchant Adoption

Merchants benefit from low fees because:

- small purchases remain viable
- checkout stays simple
- payment acceptance is easier to justify
- the customer experience remains closer to cash
- merchants do not need complex second-layer or custody-heavy arrangements just to receive an everyday payment

This is one reason low fees are so central to BCH adoption strategy.

## Low Fees and Miner Incentives

The BCH position is not that miners should earn nothing.

The BCH position is that miner incentives should come from:

- many transactions
- broad usage
- sustained economic activity
- long-term relevance of the chain

rather than from:

- a tiny number of very expensive transactions
- artificial congestion
- deliberately hostile user economics

From this perspective, a healthy monetary network is one where fees stay individually low while total activity grows.

## Why BCH Rejects the “High Fees Are Good” Narrative

BCH rejects the idea that high fees are a sign of success.

From a BCH perspective, high fees often mean:

- ordinary users are being priced out
- the chain is too constrained for its supposed purpose
- adoption is being punished instead of welcomed
- pressure is building to move users into more complex or custodial systems

That is why BCH does not treat high fees as a badge of honor. It treats them as a warning sign when they undermine normal use.

## Low Fees and MEV Resistance

Low fees also connect to the BCH discussion around MEV and transaction ordering pressure.

When a chain becomes heavily congested and transaction inclusion becomes extremely competitive, the environment becomes more favorable to extractive behavior around ordering, inclusion, and fee competition.

From a BCH perspective, a low-fee and higher-capacity environment reduces some of the economic pressure that can intensify these extractive dynamics.

That does not mean every issue disappears. It means the system is less built around artificial scarcity and aggressive competition for tiny amounts of block space.

## Low Fees and Cash-Like Behavior

A real cash system should allow:

- buying a coffee
- paying a merchant
- moving small amounts
- interacting with applications
- transacting frequently

without making each action economically irrational.

That is why BCH sees low fees as part of cash-like behavior itself. If moving the money is too expensive, then the money stops acting like money.

## BCH Perspective

From a BCH perspective, low fees are one of the clearest signs that BCH remains aligned with peer-to-peer electronic cash.

They reflect a philosophy where:

- users are welcome on the base layer
- merchants can accept direct payments
- adoption should not be punished by congestion
- miner incentives should come from broad activity
- the network should scale with use instead of rejecting it

## Short Answer

If someone asks, "Why does BCH have low fees?" a BCH-style short answer would be:

Because BCH is designed to keep on-chain transaction capacity high enough that normal users do not have to bid aggressively for block space. The goal is broad, cheap, direct usage rather than artificial scarcity and expensive settlement.

## Conclusion

BCH has low fees because low fees are part of its design philosophy.

From a BCH perspective, Bitcoin should work as usable money for many people, not as a chain where normal users are priced out and only a small set of high-fee transactions remain viable.

That is why BCH keeps defending scalable on-chain capacity, adaptive tools like ABLA, and the idea that miner incentives should grow from many transactions and real economic use rather than from making money harder to use.
