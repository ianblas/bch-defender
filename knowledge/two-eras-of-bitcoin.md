# The Two Eras of Bitcoin

## Summary

The idea of the **two eras of Bitcoin** is a framework for understanding how miner incentives change over time.

In the first era, miners are mainly funded by inflation through the block subsidy. In the second era, miners must increasingly be funded by actual economic activity on the network.

This distinction matters because it changes how miners think, what they care about, how involved they become, and whether they defend a model of Bitcoin based on long-term network usefulness or short-term subsidy dependence.

From a BCH perspective, this framework helps explain why scaling matters, why transaction volume matters, and why a system that limits its own usefulness may fail in the long run.

## Core Idea

Bitcoin does not operate under one permanent incentive structure.

It passes through two different eras:

- an early era dominated by block subsidy
- a later era dominated by network activity and transaction fees

The central claim is that these two eras produce different miner behavior.

As long as inflation is doing most of the work, many miners can remain passive, short-term oriented, and less interested in the long-term health of the system. But as subsidy declines, miners must increasingly care about whether the network is actually useful, active, and capable of sustaining security through real usage.

## Why This Matters

A key point in the episode is that Bitcoin cannot succeed merely through branding, ideology, or temporary price appreciation.

It must succeed as a functioning economic system.

That means the network needs:

- real activity
- real users
- many transactions
- a sustainable fee base
- incentives aligned with long-term usefulness

In the visible transcript, the discussion emphasizes that Bitcoin requires many transactions to function properly and remain secure, and that outside the original peer-to-peer cash model, the system does not reach its intended destination.

## The First Era

## Main characteristic

In the first era, the main source of miner revenue is **inflation**, meaning the block subsidy.

This creates a very different environment from the one Bitcoin must face later.

## Features of the first era

### 1. Most miner income comes from inflation

In this stage, miners are mostly paid through newly issued coins rather than through economic activity on the network.

This means the system can continue functioning even if the relationship between real network usage and miner incentives is still weak.

### 2. Only a minority of miners are “awake”

A minority of miners fully understand or care about the long-term transition.

Many miners can remain passive because subsidy revenue is still doing the heavy lifting.

### 3. Miners do not yet depend on the post-inflation model

Because they are still mainly funded by block rewards, many miners do not need to think seriously about whether the network will remain viable when inflation fades.

That weakens their incentive to engage with deeper structural questions.

### 4. Miners tend not to get involved

In the first era, miners often remain relatively disengaged from the broader strategic direction of the system.

They may focus on routine operations and avoid major threats, but they do not necessarily take an active role in defending the long-term economic model.

### 5. Short-term thinking dominates

If present income is mainly coming from subsidy, then the system encourages shorter time horizons.

This makes it easier for many participants to ignore future problems.

### 6. Most miners do not focus on scaling

Because the network is still being carried by inflation, many miners do not yet see scaling as an urgent existential issue.

They may support defensive actions against obvious attacks, but they do not necessarily prioritize expanding the network’s economic capacity.

### 7. Developers hold more power

When miners are passive and not deeply involved, developers can gain more influence over the direction of the network.

This is especially true if miners are not yet acting as conscious defenders of their own long-term incentives.

### 8. The focus is limited to avoiding major attacks

In the first era, the system may still be defended against blatant threats, but without a serious push toward the scaling and utility required for the future.

That creates a fragile equilibrium.

## The Transition Between Eras

The transition is the most politically and economically important phase.

It is the period in which miners begin to realize that they cannot depend forever on inflation, and that the long-term success of Bitcoin depends on actual network activity.

## What changes during the transition

### 1. More miners begin to “claim their throne”

As subsidy becomes less central over time, more miners begin to understand that their future depends on the actual functioning of the system.

They become less passive and more conscious of their role.

### 2. Developers resist losing power

A major part of this framework is that not everyone benefits equally from miner awakening.

If miners become more active and more aligned with long-term network incentives, developers lose some of the informal power they held during the passive era.

This creates conflict.

### 3. Permanent inflation becomes tempting

One of the warnings in this framework is that when a system becomes dependent on subsidy, there is pressure to preserve the inflation party indefinitely.

The transcript and your notes point to this as an attempt to introduce the “poison” of permanent inflation.

From this perspective, permanent inflation is not a solution. It is an attempt to avoid the real transition.

### 4. Some actors want to preserve the artificial world

As the transition begins, some participants prefer to maintain the old arrangement:

- subsidy dependence
- passive miners
- weak alignment between usage and security
- developer-heavy control

In this sense, the transition is not just economic. It is political.

### 5. Miner behavior gradually changes

Over time, miners begin to act differently:

- they think more long-term
- they become more attentive to network health
- they become more involved in the strategic direction of the chain
- they care more about scaling and real usage

This is the beginning of the second era.

## The Second Era

## Main characteristic

In the second era, the main source of miner revenue increasingly comes from **network activity** rather than inflation.

This changes everything.

## Features of the second era

### 1. Most miner income comes from network activity

The system increasingly depends on real economic use:

- transactions
- fees
- demand for block space
- broad participation in the network

This ties miner incentives more directly to whether Bitcoin is actually useful.

### 2. Most miners are “awake”

In this era, the majority of miners understand that their future depends on the health and usefulness of the network.

They can no longer afford to remain passive.

### 3. Miners become involved

Unlike in the first era, miners begin to actively defend the conditions that sustain their future income.

That means they care more about:

- network usefulness
- scaling
- adoption
- long-term fee generation
- sound incentive design

### 4. Long-term thinking dominates

When inflation can no longer carry the system, miners must think beyond the next cycle.

Their perspective becomes more strategic and less dependent on temporary conditions.

### 5. Scaling becomes central

If the network must sustain itself through real activity, then scaling stops being optional.

It becomes necessary.

This is one of the most important implications of the entire framework: if Bitcoin is supposed to survive in the second era, it must support enough real activity to generate the economic base that secures the chain.

### 6. Miners get involved directly in development

As their interests become more exposed, miners can no longer leave everything to developers.

They begin to involve themselves more directly in development decisions, protocol direction, and defense of the economic model that supports the chain.

### 7. Developers lose power

This does not mean developers become irrelevant.

It means that in a mature system, developers cannot remain the main political center if miners are fully aware of their role and incentives.

As miners wake up, developer dominance declines.

## Why Many Transactions Matter

One of the most important points discussed in the episode is that Bitcoin needs many transactions in order to function successfully in the long run.

The argument is not merely that “more transactions are nice to have.”

The argument is that without a large volume of real activity:

- miner revenue becomes too dependent on disappearing subsidy
- security becomes fragile
- the economic base of the network remains too thin
- the system fails to reach the peer-to-peer cash model described by Satoshi

In this framing, scaling is not cosmetic. It is structural.

## Why the BCH Perspective Cares About This Framework

From a BCH perspective, the two-era model helps explain why BCH places so much emphasis on:

- on-chain scaling
- low fees
- broad transaction volume
- merchant use
- real economic activity
- alignment between usage and security

The claim is that Bitcoin can only succeed in the second era if it becomes economically useful enough to sustain itself through actual activity.

If a chain limits its own transaction capacity and suppresses broad usage, then it enters the second era with a weak foundation.

## The Conflict Between the Two Visions

This framework also explains a broader conflict.

One vision says:

- Bitcoin can remain constrained
- high fees are acceptable or even desirable
- real usage can move elsewhere
- the system will somehow remain secure anyway

The other vision says:

- Bitcoin must remain usable as peer-to-peer electronic cash
- real activity must remain on-chain in meaningful volume
- scaling is essential
- miner security must be tied to real economic usefulness

From the BCH point of view, only the second vision is sustainable in the long run.

## Conclusion

The two eras of Bitcoin describe the transition from an inflation-funded network to an activity-funded network.

In the first era, miners can remain passive because subsidy still dominates. In the second era, miners must care deeply about scaling, real usage, and long-term network health.

This matters because Bitcoin cannot depend forever on inflation. If it is going to survive as a secure monetary system, it must eventually be sustained by the activity of the network itself.

That is why this framework leads to a clear conclusion from a BCH perspective:

A Bitcoin that does not scale into broad economic use cannot successfully enter its second era.
