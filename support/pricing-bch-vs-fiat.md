# Pricing BCH vs Fiat

## Summary

Most merchants should start by keeping their product prices in local fiat and converting to BCH at checkout.

This is usually the simplest operational model.

## Why It Matters

A business already thinks in terms of costs, margins, and replacement prices.

For most merchants, BCH is easier to adopt when:

- core pricing stays familiar
- BCH is used as the payment rail
- the converted BCH amount is shown at the moment of sale

## Recommended Starting Model

### Use fiat as the pricing reference

For example:

- coffee price stays in pesos or dollars
- checkout tool converts that value into BCH
- customer pays the BCH amount shown

This reduces confusion for:

- staff
- customers
- accounting
- restocking decisions

## When to Quote Directly in BCH

Direct BCH pricing can make sense when:

- the business already thinks in BCH terms
- the community around the business is BCH-native
- the merchant wants to strengthen circular use consciously

But that is usually a later-stage choice, not the best first step for most businesses.

## Common Mistakes

- changing the pricing rule depending on whoever is at the register
- not deciding whether the source reference is fiat or BCH
- using inconsistent exchange references
- expecting new staff to improvise conversions mentally

## Short Answer

For most merchants, the best starting point is simple: keep prices in fiat and convert to BCH at checkout. That preserves normal business logic while still letting BCH function as the payment method.
