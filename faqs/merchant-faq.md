# Merchant FAQ

## Why would a merchant accept BCH?

Common reasons include low payment fees, no chargebacks, access to global customers, and direct control over settlement.

## Are BCH payment fees high?

Typically, BCH fees are very low relative to card processing fees. Exact fee levels vary by wallet settings and network conditions, but BCH is generally used for low-cost transfers.

## Can customers charge back BCH payments?

At the protocol level, no. Confirmed BCH transactions are not reversible like card chargebacks. Merchants should still maintain clear refund policies.

## Should prices be shown in fiat or BCH?

Either can work:
- Many merchants keep catalog prices in fiat and convert at checkout.
- Some communities also use direct BCH pricing.

Fiat reference pricing is common for businesses managing accounting and volatility.

## How do merchants get started quickly?

1. Choose a BCH wallet or payment processor.
2. Set checkout method (QR code, POS app, or plugin).
3. Decide settlement policy (hold BCH, auto-convert, or mixed).
4. Train staff on payment confirmation basics.
5. Publish "BCH accepted" in-store and online.

## What about refunds?

Refunds are merchant-initiated. Best practice is to:
- verify the refund address carefully
- record transaction IDs
- define refund windows and terms clearly

## Is BCH difficult to integrate?

Not usually. Small merchants can start with a wallet and QR code. Larger merchants can use APIs, processors, or e-commerce plugins.

## What are the main risks merchants should plan for?

- Price volatility if holding BCH
- Operational mistakes (wrong address, poor staff process)
- Incomplete accounting workflow

These are manageable with clear procedures and proper tooling.
