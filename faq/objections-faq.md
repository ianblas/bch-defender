# Objections FAQ

## Is BCH dead?

No.

BCH still exists as a functioning peer-to-peer payment network with real wallet use, merchant use, and self-custodial utility.

A better question is whether BCH is useful for direct payments. For many users and merchants, that answer is yes.

## Why not just use BTC?

BTC and BCH prioritize different things.

BTC is often framed more as a settlement or store-of-value asset. BCH is more directly focused on usable peer-to-peer cash and everyday payments.

If the goal is practical direct payments, BCH has a clearer fit.

## Why should a merchant care about BCH?

Because BCH can offer:

- low fees
- direct payment
- no mandatory chargebacks when accepted directly
- a simple wallet-and-QR checkout flow

For some merchants, that is operationally useful even before ideology enters the conversation.

## Isn’t volatility too high?

Volatility is real, but it is not the whole picture.

A merchant can reduce operational volatility by pricing in local fiat and converting to BCH at checkout. The question is not whether volatility exists, but whether the payment rail is still useful.

## Why not just use cards?

Cards are familiar, but they are not the same thing.

BCH allows direct peer-to-peer payment without depending on the traditional card stack, chargeback model, or banking rails in the same way.

## Isn’t zero-conf unsafe?

Zero-conf should be understood as a practical retail tool, not as a universal rule for every transaction.

For ordinary low-value in-person payments, BCH merchants can often accept visible payments quickly. Larger or higher-risk payments may justify stricter handling.

## Why would customers use BCH?

Some use it because they want direct digital cash. Some want low fees. Some want self-custody. Some already live in BCH communities or use it for practical payments.

The point is not that every customer must use BCH. The point is that BCH gives customers another payment option with different strengths.

## Isn’t self-custody too hard for normal people?

It can feel unfamiliar at first, but unfamiliar is not the same as impossible.

Most users do not need to learn everything at once. They need a clear wallet, a safe backup habit, and a simple first payment flow.

## Why not just use stablecoins instead?

Stablecoins solve a different problem.

They may reduce price volatility, but they often come with different trust assumptions, chain dependencies, custodial risks, or platform dependencies. BCH remains attractive when the goal is direct, self-custodial peer-to-peer cash.

## Nobody uses BCH. Why bother?

That objection usually confuses “not universal” with “not useful.”

A payment method does not need to be universal to be valuable. If BCH works for a real group of users, merchants, and communities, that already matters.

## Isn’t crypto too complicated for everyday payments?

It can be if explained badly.

But a simple BCH flow can be operationally straightforward: open wallet, scan QR, confirm amount, tap Send, verify payment.

## What makes BCH practical for payments?

Its practical value comes from combining:

- direct peer-to-peer transfer
- low fees
- self-custodial control
- fast everyday payment usability

That combination is what makes BCH worth considering as money rather than just as an abstract crypto asset.
